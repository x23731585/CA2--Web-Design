Science-y part

Alright, get ready for a rollercoaster of facts because here comes the science and trust me, it's more fun than a barrel of lab monkeys!

Filter Feeding

Picture this: a solo littleneck-sized clam, no bigger than your palm, can single-handedly filter a whopping 4.5 gallons of seawater each day. Isn't that clam-tastic?But how do they do it? Well, as they're munching away, they create currents that shuttle water in and out. Their gills, which are covered in teeny-tiny moving cilia (think super small hair-like structures), pump water through the clam. It's like a mini water treatment plant!These cilia are quite the multitaskers, catching all the suspended particles in the water, and then, like a conveyor belt, moving them straight to the clam's mouth. Nom nom!Then, voila! The water, now cleared of all those particles, is ejected back out. Thanks to these hardworking clams, the water is now cleaner, with less sediment and excess nutrients. It's like they're the janitors of the sea!The result? Clearer water that allows more sunlight to reach the depths, aiding the growth of seagrasses and boosting oxygen levels. And guess what? These filter-feeding champions might even help prevent those pesky harmful algal blooms.

Removing Nitrogen

Now, clams aren't going around sipping nitrogen from the water directly. Oh no, they're way too clever for that! They rather chow down on the naturally-occurring phytoplankton, which have already done the hard work of using the dissolved inorganic nitrogen in the water to grow. Talk about smart dining!As they munch away, clams store nitrogen from their food in their tissues and shells. And here's the kicker: when we harvest these tasty critters, we're also removing the accumulated nitrogen from the water. It's a win-win!But wait, there's more! Clams are not just nitrogen hoarders, they're also part of the nutrient recycling program. They release nitrogenous waste (yep, clam pee) that the phytoplankton can use as a nitrogen source. Talk about giving back to the community!And let's not forget their contribution to the ocean floor. Some of the nitrogen they filter from the water gets deposited onto the sediment as feces and pseudofeces (a fancy word for rejected food particles). These biodeposits are decomposed by bacteria, transforming the nitrogen into a variety of other forms, including ammonium, nitrate, and nitrogen gas.So next time you see a clam, give it a little nod of thanks for its hard work in keeping our coastal waters clean!

Sequestering Carbon

Clams are the superheroes we never knew we needed! They're out here, just doing their thing, pulling a Houdini on carbon from the atmosphere. One clam,can stash away a whopping 2.76 grams of atmospheric carbon. How, you ask? They turn carbon into their swanky calcium carbonate shells. Most of the carbonate they use comes from atmospheric CO2 that's dissolved in seawater. So, these cultured clams are basically running a long-term carbon storage unit.But hold on, it gets better. Clams don’t just store carbon in their shells and tissues, they're also processing it while they're growing. Think of them as little factories, churning out carbon dioxide as a byproduct of respiration - just like us, really.And let's not forget about the carbon that ends up in the sediments as feces and pseudofeces. Yeah, it's a bit gross, but it's a clam-eat-clam world out there. This carbon is a buffet for critters like worms, brittle stars, and other deposit feeders. And some of this carbon? It's going to stick around for the long haul, locked in the sediment as shell fragments, limestone, and dolomite.Meanwhile, most of the carbon in plant and animal tissues is outta there, back to carbon dioxide within a few years. So, here’s to the clams, our hard-shell carbon capturers!
